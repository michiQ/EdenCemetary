Why Eden Matters

“You cannot do Black Philly history without Eden Cemetery.”

— Community workshop, 2026
Listed on the National Register of Historic Places and a National Underground Railroad Network to Freedom site.

Philadelphia was the civic laboratory of Black America — the city where Black people invented how to protest, how to claim rights, how to build institutions that could sustain a community under conditions of profound oppression. The people who did that are buried at Eden.

When Lebanon and Olive Cemeteries — home to Philadelphia’s Black citizens — were forced to close, Eden absorbed them all. It now holds the abolitionists who operated the Underground Railroad in plain sight, the ministers who built the first Black churches, the poets who told America it could not be free while it held people in chains, the architects who designed the buildings of elite American institutions, and the musicians who carried the sorrow and joy of a people to the world’s great stages.

When you walk Eden’s 53 acres, you are not visiting a cemetery. You are time traveling through the foundation of Black American identity.

History ignored is history repeated. Stories = History. Past = Now.

Our Story · Beginnings

Founded so that death would not be another site of dispossession.

The creation of the Eden Cemetery Company was a collaborative act of African American agency: a sanctuary where Black Philadelphians could be buried with dignity and respect, founded by Jacob C. White Sr. and managed by his son, Jacob C. White Jr., at the height of Jim Crow — six years after Plessy v. Ferguson — in answer to a burial crisis created by segregation, urban expansion, condemnation, and the closure of the community’s earlier burial grounds.

The Collection Cemetery

Lebanon. Olive. Eden held them all.

The great Black burial grounds of Philadelphia — home to some of the most important Black Americans in history — were forced to close. Other cemeteries were not forced to move. Only theirs. Even in death, the mistreatment continued.

But Eden held them. Their residents were reinterred on Eden’s grounds, where the cemetery’s sections became declarations of pride — like the John Brown section, the chosen resting place of United States Colored Troops and of Frances Ellen Watkins Harper, who counted Brown a friend and hero.

What looks like a cemetery is actually an act of resistance — 124 years of refusing to let Black history be buried twice.

Musicians & Performers

Marian Anderson

1897 – 1993

One of the greatest voices of the twentieth century. When the Daughters of the American Revolution barred her from Constitution Hall in 1939, she sang instead from the steps of the Lincoln Memorial before 75,000 people — a moment that became a landmark of the civil rights movement. In 1955 she became the first African American to perform with the Metropolitan Opera.

She carried the sorrow and joy of a people to the world’s great stages. She rests at Eden.

Civic Leaders · The Forgotten Hero

Octavius V. Catto

1839 – 1871

Scholar, educator, and the dominant civil rights leader of his generation. Catto led efforts to register thousands of African Americans to vote, desegregated Philadelphia’s streetcars, and served as a major in the Pennsylvania National Guard. He was assassinated on Election Day, October 10, 1871, in front of a polling place on South Street — a martyr for the ballot, a century before Selma.

Reinterred from Lebanon Cemetery — Eden held him. Eden holds him still.
